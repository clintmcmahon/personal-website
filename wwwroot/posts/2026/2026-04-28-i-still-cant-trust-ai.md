I have a coffee subscription where I get a new coffee every week. To minimize the dialing in of new coffees, I started using AI to help. I have a Claude project with my fixed parameters that give context to what I'm working with: a Chemex pour over, Baratza grinder and a Fellow gooseneck kettle. All I do is take a picture of the bag and ask Claude how I should brew it. It spits out the grind size, water temperature and pour timing relative to my fixed parameters. This works reasonably well. When a cup comes out bitter I'd say so and the system would adjust. Coarser grind, different ratio. Usually pointing in the right direction.

This all works pretty great until a coffee that I've brewed before doesn't match up with what Claude has produced from previous pours of the same coffee. I started to notice that Claude was giving me different brewing parameters for a specific SEY Ethiopian roast I've brewed before. Each chat had its own context and revealed different brewing recipes. This is a common drawback with LLMs, you never get the same exact result twice. Ask the same question twice and you get two different answers.

This is fine, but when I got two different results for the same coffee I pushed back on Claude about the difference and it changed its answer. I asked "are you sure?" and suddenly we were in "You're absolutely correct!" territory.

It bothers me that it does this whether I'm right or wrong. If the answer was correct and I question it, the system will give me a different answer that isn't correct. Just to make me happy.

My pushback didn't reveal a hidden truth. It just changed the output.

I only caught this because I already know this coffee. But most people using AI aren't subject matter experts in what they're asking about. A lot of times people are using AI because they don't already know the answer. Those are the people taking the first answer. And the system sounds confident either way.

AI systems are a work in progress and they are getting better. But they are not yet 100% accurate and maybe they never will be. There has to be someone who already knows enough to catch the mistake.
