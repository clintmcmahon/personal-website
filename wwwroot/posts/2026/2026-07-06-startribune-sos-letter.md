Working on government software is mostly invisible. You do the migration, fix the redirects, make the slow pages fast, make the confusing navigation less confusing — and then you move on. The work just exists. Nobody writes about it.

A few days ago I was reading the Star Tribune and came across a letter in the opinion section. The headline was "Bravo to hardworking staffers" — an open letter to Secretary of State Steve Simon and everyone in his office. The writer said she'd never paid attention to the Secretary of State's office before. Then:

> "Its website is second-to-none in readability and ease of use, and I learned a lot."

I worked on the rebuild of that site.

I sat with that for a minute.

The project was a migration from Umbraco 7 to 14 and .NET Framework to .NET Core. Months of work — a lot of it unglamorous. Content migrations, redirect maps, performance testing, making sure things didn't break when you resized the browser window. The kind of work you do because it needs doing.

The woman who wrote that letter doesn't know any of that. She just needed to find something on a government website, found it, and felt grateful enough to write a letter to the newspaper. That's the whole point. That's what you're actually trying to do.

I don't think I've ever had a stranger thank me for software I built. Not like this. A comment somewhere, maybe. But a letter in the Star Tribune — addressed to the people who made a website work — that hit differently.

I'm not going to pretend I don't feel good about it.
